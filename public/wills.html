Wills & Estate Basics

A will generally sets out who should receive your estate and who is responsible for the administration. This page is general information only — not legal, tax or financial advice.

What a Will Typically Covers

  • Names executors to administer the estate.
  • Sets out beneficiaries and how assets are intended to be shared.
  • May include guardianship wishes for children (subject to legal processes).
  • Can reference personal items, gifts and charitable legacies.

Keeping a Will Valid & Current

  • Signing and witnessing must meet the legal requirements in your jurisdiction.
  • People often review wills after life events (marriage, civil partnership, separation, children, property changes).
  • Store the signed original safely and tell executors where it is.
  • Some assets can pass outside a will (for example, certain jointly-owned assets or nominated-beneficiary accounts).

If unsure, consider a qualified solicitor or professional will writer.

If There Is No Will (Intestacy)

Without a valid will, the estate is usually distributed under intestacy rules. Outcomes may differ from personal intentions and can take time to resolve.
