Whole of Life Insurance

Whole of life insurance is designed to stay in force for the rest of your life and pay a lump sum whenever you die (as long as premiums are maintained). It’s often used to help with funeral costs, gifts to family, or estate planning. Information only — not financial advice.

What it is (Plain English)

  • Cover is intended to last your whole life, not a fixed term.
  • A lump sum is paid when you die, provided the policy is in force at that time.
  • Premiums are usually paid monthly; depending on the policy, they can be guaranteed or reviewable.

Key Features & Options

  • Guaranteed vs reviewable premiums: Guaranteed stay fixed (unless you choose indexation); reviewable may change over time.
  • Indexation: Option to increase your cover each year to help with inflation (premiums typically increase too).
  • Guaranteed whole of life (GWoL): Often used for funeral provision or leaving a set amount to beneficiaries.
  • Joint life options: For couples, policies can be set up as joint life first-death or second-death (often used in estate planning).
  • Trusts: Writing the policy in trust can help benefits go to the right people quickly and may assist with estate administration.

Whole of Life vs Term Assurance

  • Whole of Life: Designed to pay out whenever death occurs (premiums required throughout or to a specified paid-up point). Useful where a payout is expected to be needed whenever death happens.
  • Term Assurance: Covers you for a set period only (e.g., 20–30 years). Often used for mortgage protection or while children are financially dependent.

People sometimes combine products — for example, a term policy for mortgage/children plus whole of life for final expenses or legacy planning.

Things to Consider (Information Only)

  • Affordability over time: Premiums are paid for a long period; reviewable premiums and indexation can increase cost.
  • Purpose: Clarify if the aim is funeral provision, a gift/legacy, or part of wider estate planning.
  • Trusts & beneficiaries: Consider whether a trust is appropriate so the payment goes as intended.
  • Underwriting: Health and lifestyle can affect terms, exclusions, or whether cover is offered.

Deeper Detail (For those who want more)

  • Paid-up or limited-pay options: Some policies allow premiums for a fixed period (e.g., 10/20 years), after which cover continues.
  • With-profits/unit-linked variants: Some legacy products link to investments and can have variable values/premiums.
  • Second-death policies: Sometimes used alongside estate planning; timing and ownership/trust setup can matter.
