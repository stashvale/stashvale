Whole of Life Insurance

Whole of Life insurance is designed to pay out whenever you die, rather than for a fixed term. It’s often used for long-term planning — such as helping cover inheritance tax liabilities or leaving a guaranteed lump sum. Information only — not advice or a personal recommendation. Rules, products and tax treatment can change.

How It Works

  • Unlike term insurance, Whole of Life cover has no end date (as long as premiums are maintained).
  • The policy pays out the agreed lump sum on death — whenever that occurs.
  • Premiums are typically higher than term insurance, reflecting the guaranteed nature of the cover.
  • Many policies have guaranteed premiums, but some are reviewable or investment-linked.

Example: A policy taken at age 50 may remain in place for life, paying a fixed or reviewable premium until death, at which point the lump sum is paid to beneficiaries.

Key Features & Options

  • Guaranteed or reviewable premiums: Guaranteed gives certainty; reviewable may change over time.
  • Single or joint life: Joint policies typically pay out on the first death, but second death options exist (useful in estate planning).
  • Trusts: Placing the policy in trust can help speed up payment to beneficiaries and may help with estate planning.
  • Indexation: Option to increase the sum assured over time to help protect against inflation.
  • Investment-linked options: Some policies are linked to investments and may have variable premiums or benefits.

Things to Consider (Information Only)

  • Purpose of cover: Common uses include estate planning, funeral costs or leaving a legacy.
  • Affordability over time: Because cover runs for life, it’s essential to ensure premiums are sustainable.
  • Trusts & tax planning: Trusts can be useful to help avoid delays in probate and potentially reduce inheritance tax implications.
  • Premium reviews: If the policy has reviewable premiums, costs may increase later in life.
  • Inflation: Fixed sums can lose value in real terms. Indexation can help maintain purchasing power.
  • Underwriting & disclosure: Accurate and complete medical/lifestyle disclosure is required. Non-disclosure can impact claims.
  • Regulated advice: This type of cover often involves complex tax and estate considerations. Consider regulated financial and legal advice.
