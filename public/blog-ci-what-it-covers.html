Critical Illness: What It Actually Covers

Information only — not advice. Critical illness cover (CIC) usually pays a benefit if a listed medical condition meets the policy’s definition during the term. The exact wording varies by insurer and by product version.

How policies typically work

  • A claim is considered when a specified condition is diagnosed to the severity defined in the wording.
  • Most policies pay a lump sum; many also include partial payments for lower-severity definitions.
  • Waiting or survival periods and exclusions can apply, depending on the policy.

Insurer definitions are decisive. Names of conditions can look similar across providers while criteria differ.

Commonly listed conditions (examples)

Policies often include certain cancers, heart attack, stroke, multiple sclerosis, major organ transplant/failure and a selection of neurological conditions. Each condition has its own definition and evidence requirements.

Partial payments & additional features

Many modern CIC plans pay a smaller benefit for specified early-stage diagnoses. Some products include or offer children’s cover with separate limits and eligible ages. Availability and amounts vary by insurer.

What’s not usually covered

  • Conditions not listed or that don’t meet the severity definition.
  • Pre-existing conditions where excluded.
  • Events outside the policy term, or where premiums weren’t maintained.

Underwriting & disclosure

Applications typically ask about health, lifestyle and family history. Accurate disclosure helps the insurer assess eligibility and price. Non-disclosure can affect future claims.

How claims usually progress

  1. Notify the insurer and provide requested medical evidence.
  2. The claim is assessed against the policy definition.
  3. If approved, the benefit is paid according to the rules (full or partial).
