What Is Key Person Insurance?

A plain-English overview of how key person (key man / key woman) insurance works, why some companies consider it, and common points to check. This article is for information only — it is not financial, legal, accounting, or tax advice.

The Basic Idea

Key person insurance is a life (and sometimes critical illness) policy taken out by a business on an individual whose skills, knowledge, relationships, or revenue-generation are considered critical to the company. The business is usually the policy owner, pays the premiums, and is the beneficiary of any payout.

Who Might Be a “Key Person”?

  • Founders and senior executives who drive strategy or hold specialist know-how.
  • Highly technical staff whose expertise is hard to replace quickly.
  • Top salespeople or rainmakers linked to major accounts.
  • Individuals essential to product development, compliance, or operations.

Each business defines “key” differently. Identification usually comes from impact on revenue, profitability, continuity, or financing obligations.

What Cover Can Do (Typical Uses of a Payout)

  • Provide working capital while recruiting and onboarding a replacement.
  • Offset lost revenue or increased operating costs during disruption.
  • Support loan repayments or meet covenants if lenders require continuity plans.
  • Stabilise cash flow for suppliers and payroll during a transition period.

Actual use of proceeds is a commercial decision for the company and may be influenced by loan agreements or shareholder expectations.

Common Policy Structures & Options (Information Only)

  • Life only vs life with critical illness: Some companies include critical illness; others keep it to life cover only.
  • Term length: Often aligned with forecast replacement timeframes, project cycles, debt terms, or investor requirements.
  • Sum assured: Sometimes estimated from multiples of profit contribution, gross profit, or replacement costs. Methods vary.
  • Premium basis: Level or reviewable, depending on the product.
  • Ownership & beneficiary: Typically the company; wording should reflect commercial intent.

Tax, Accounting & Legal Notes (High Level Only)

  • Tax: Treatment of premiums and proceeds can depend on purpose, policy terms and local rules. Tax rules can change.
  • Accounting: Presentation may differ by jurisdiction and standards. Professional accounting advice is recommended.
  • Legal: Board approvals, employment status, and insurable interest considerations can apply.
  • Underwriting: Medical and financial underwriting normally applies. Non-disclosure can affect claims.

Businesses commonly obtain professional advice (tax, legal, accounting and regulated insurance advice) before proceeding.

Key Points to Remember

  • “Key person” is a commercial concept; needs and sums assured vary by business.
  • Policy terms, definitions, and exclusions differ by provider and product.
  • Tax and accounting treatment is specific to circumstances and jurisdiction.
  • This guide is educational only and does not recommend any product or structure.
