How Family Income Benefit Works

Family Income Benefit (FIB) is an insurance policy that, if the insured person dies during the policy term, pays a regular, tax-free income to the chosen beneficiaries for the remainder of that term. This guide explains the main mechanics in plain English. This page is for information only — not financial advice.

The Basics

  • Purpose: Provide ongoing income rather than a single lump sum.
  • Policy term: A fixed number of years (e.g., until children reach financial independence).
  • Benefit: A monthly (or yearly) payout that runs until the end of the term if a valid claim occurs.
  • Premiums: Paid monthly. Cost depends on age, health, smoker status, length of term, benefit level, and policy features.

How the Payout Works

If a valid claim happens within the term, the policy pays the insured income for the rest of that term. For example, if a 20-year policy is set up for £1,800 per month and a valid claim occurs in year 7, the income is typically paid for the remaining 13 years.

  • Level vs. indexed income: Some policies allow the income to rise each year (e.g., in line with inflation caps). Indexation usually increases premiums over time.
  • Guaranteed vs. reviewable premiums: Guaranteed premiums remain fixed (apart from indexation). Reviewable premiums can change under the insurer’s rules.
  • Deferred periods: FIB usually doesn’t have a deferred period (unlike income protection); payments start after claim approval.

Setting the Term & Amount (Information Only)

People often align the term to key milestones — for example, the youngest child’s likely independence age. The monthly amount is typically chosen to help cover essential outgoings. This is general information only; individual circumstances vary widely.

Claims & Trusts (General Information)

  • Making a claim: A representative/beneficiary submits a claim with required evidence (e.g., death certificate). Insurers assess claims against policy terms and definitions.
  • Using a trust: Many people place policies in trust so proceeds are paid to the intended beneficiaries efficiently. Trust options and effects can vary and may have legal/tax implications.
  • Tax: UK tax treatment can depend on personal circumstances and may change. Professional guidance may be appropriate for tax questions.

Key Points to Remember

  • Policy terms, exclusions, and definitions vary by insurer and product version.
  • Missing premiums usually leads to the policy ending without value.
  • Regular reviews can help keep cover aligned to life events (e.g., children growing up).
