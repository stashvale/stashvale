Whole of Life vs Term — What’s the Difference?

This guide explains, in plain English, how whole of life and term life insurance differ. It is general information only — not advice and not a recommendation.

At a Glance

  • Whole of Life (WOL): Aims to cover your entire lifetime. A payout is guaranteed on death (if premiums are maintained and terms are met).
  • Term Life: Covers a fixed period (e.g., 10–30 years). It pays out only if death occurs during the policy term.
  • Cost: WOL typically has higher overall cost than like-for-like term because cover lasts for life.
  • Common uses: WOL is often linked to estate planning or funeral planning; term is often linked to income/loan/mortgage needs within a time frame.

How Payouts Work

  • Whole of Life: Pays a lump sum when the insured person dies, whenever that occurs, provided the policy is in force.
  • Term Life: Pays a lump sum if death happens within the selected term. No payout if the term ends without a claim.

Policy definitions, exclusions and underwriting can affect claims. Always read the policy documents.

Premium Patterns (High Level)

  • Whole of Life: Premiums may be guaranteed (fixed) or reviewable (periodically reassessed under the policy terms). See our premiums guide for more detail.
  • Term Life: Premiums are commonly guaranteed for the term. Some policies allow limited reviewability under set conditions.

Common Use Cases (Information Only)

  • Whole of Life: Some people use it within wider estate planning, or for funeral-cost planning. Ownership/trust structure can matter for timing and who receives the payout.
  • Term Life: Often aligned to time-bound needs (e.g., supporting children to independence or covering a mortgage term).

Tax treatment and suitability depend on individual circumstances and may change. Professional advice can help with tax/estate planning.

Key Things to Remember

  • WOL aims to last for life; term aims to last for a set period.
  • Payouts depend on the policy being in force and meeting definitions and conditions.
  • Premium type (guaranteed vs reviewable) affects price certainty over time.
  • Trusts and ownership can influence how quickly and to whom proceeds are paid.

Illustrative Examples (Not Advice)

  • Example A: Someone planning for potential funeral costs in later life might look at WOL to provide a lifelong lump sum on death.
  • Example B: Someone wanting cover until their children are financially independent might look at a 20–25-year term policy.

These are generic examples to explain concepts. Individual needs vary.
