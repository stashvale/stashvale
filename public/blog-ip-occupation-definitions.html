Occupation Definitions Explained

This article explains common Income Protection occupation definitions and why they matter. Information only — not advice.

Why Definitions Matter

The occupation definition describes the circumstances in which a claim may be considered. It affects eligibility and how claims are assessed. Always check the wording in the specific policy.

Common Definitions (Plain English)

  • Own occupation: A claim is generally assessed on whether you can carry out the material and substantial duties of your own job as normally performed. This is commonly preferred where available, but availability and wording vary.
  • Suited occupation: Assessment may consider whether you could reasonably perform another role suited to your education, training and experience.
  • Any occupation / work tasks: Assessment can consider whether you can perform any work, or specific tasks (e.g., lifting, standing, keyboard work). Wording differs by product.

Names and criteria differ between providers. Always read the policy terms.

Other Factors That Can Influence Claims

  • Part-time or proportionate benefits: Some policies may pay a reduced benefit if you can return in a limited capacity. Terms vary.
  • Exclusions: Pre-existing conditions, hazardous hobbies or specific medical exclusions may apply depending on underwriting.
  • Definition changes: In some policies the definition can change after a period of claim (for example, shifting from “own occupation” to a broader test). Check the wording.

Illustrative Examples (Not Advice)

  • Example A (Own occupation): A graphic designer unable to carry out the key duties of their role due to a hand injury may be considered under the policy’s “own occupation” wording, subject to terms.
  • Example B (Suited occupation): A former engineer might be considered for roles compatible with experience and training if the policy uses a “suited” definition.

Key Takeaways

  • The occupation definition is central to how IP claims are assessed.
  • Wording varies; some policies may change the definition after a set time on claim.
  • Review exclusions, underwriting outcomes and all policy documents to understand what is and isn’t covered.
