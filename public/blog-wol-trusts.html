Should Whole of Life Be in Trust? (Information Only)

This guide explains common reasons people consider using a trust with a whole of life policy. It is general information only — not legal, tax or financial advice.

What Is a Trust in This Context?

A trust is a legal arrangement that can allow policy proceeds to be paid to chosen beneficiaries, usually without forming part of the estate for probate. Different trust types exist and have different rules.

Why Some People Consider a Trust

  • Beneficiary certainty: Indicates who should receive the proceeds.
  • Potentially faster access: Proceeds held under a valid trust may be paid without waiting for probate, depending on the trust and circumstances.
  • Estate planning: Trusts can support wider planning aims. Tax treatment depends on the trust type and personal circumstances.

Common Trust Approaches (High Level)

  • Fixed/expression trusts: Beneficiaries are specified.
  • Discretionary trusts: Trustees have discretion over who benefits within a defined class, following the settlor’s wishes letter.
  • Survivorship/second-death contexts: Where policies are used alongside wills and estate planning, specialist advice is commonly sought.

The appropriate trust (if any) depends on individual goals, family situation and legal/tax considerations.

Ownership, Trustees & Control

  • Policy owner/settlor: The person(s) setting up the trust.
  • Trustees: Individuals who administer the trust according to its terms.
  • Beneficiaries: The people intended to benefit from proceeds.

Trust documentation sets out who can do what. It’s important to keep trustee and beneficiary details up to date.

Potential Drawbacks to Be Aware Of

  • Once a trust is set up, changes may be limited by the trust terms and the law.
  • Certain trust types can have tax reporting/periodic charges depending on value and jurisdiction.
  • Incorrect setup could lead to delays or outcomes different from what was intended.

Because trusts are legal arrangements with tax implications, professional advice is commonly sought.

Illustrative Examples (Not Advice)

  • Example A: A person wants policy proceeds to be available quickly to help with funeral costs. They consider using a trust so trustees can receive funds directly from the insurer.
  • Example B: A couple using WOL for estate planning consider a discretionary trust so trustees can distribute funds in line with their letter of wishes.

These are generic illustrations only. Individual goals and legal/tax positions differ.

Key Points to Remember

  • Trusts can help with payment speed and beneficiary direction, but they are legal documents with responsibilities.
  • Tax rules and product terms can change. Treatment depends on individual circumstances.
  • Specialist legal/tax advice is usually appropriate before setting up a trust.
