Critical Illness Cover

Critical illness cover pays a lump sum if you’re diagnosed with a serious condition that meets your policy’s definition. It can help with treatment costs, changes to work, or covering bills while you recover. Information only — not financial advice.

What it is (Plain English)

  • You choose an amount of cover and a term (how many years the policy runs).
  • If you’re diagnosed with a listed condition that meets the policy definition during the term, it pays out once.
  • You can buy it on its own (standalone) or combined with life insurance.

Key Types & Features

  • Standalone vs Combined: Standalone only pays for critical illness. Combined policies often pay once for either life or critical illness, depending on the claim.
  • Level vs Decreasing: Level cover keeps the payout the same; decreasing cover reduces over time (often used with repayment mortgages).
  • Additional Payments: Some policies pay smaller amounts for certain “additional” conditions without ending the policy.
  • Children’s Cover: Many insurers include optional children’s cover with set limits and listed conditions.
  • Indexation: Option for your cover (and premium) to increase annually, helping keep up with inflation.

Things to Consider (Information Only)

  • Definitions vary: Each insurer has its own wording for what counts as a claim. Always read the policy documents.
  • Scope of cover: Critical illness is not the same as income protection; it’s a one-off lump sum, not a monthly income.
  • Budget & priorities: Higher cover amounts, wider definitions, and add-ons (like children’s cover) can increase cost.
  • Term & purpose: Align the policy term to your needs (e.g., mortgage length or a key family milestone).
  • Trusts: Placing policies in trust can help benefits go to the right people; consider independent guidance.

Deeper Detail (For those who want more)

  • ABI model wordings: Many policies reference industry model definitions but can be stricter or broader.
  • Severity tiers: Some conditions pay at different severities; a diagnosis alone may not be sufficient if the criteria aren’t met.
  • Survival periods & exclusions: Some policies have minimum survival periods after diagnosis, and general exclusions (e.g., non-disclosure).
  • Underwriting: Your medical history, age, and lifestyle can impact price, exclusions, or whether cover is offered.
