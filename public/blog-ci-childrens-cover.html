Children’s Cover in Critical Illness Policies

This guide explains, in plain English, how children’s cover is commonly structured within critical illness (CI) policies. It outlines typical inclusions, limits, and exclusions. This page is for information only — not financial advice.

What Is Children’s Cover?

Many CI policies include an optional or built-in benefit that pays a lump sum if an insured child is diagnosed with a condition listed in the policy (as defined by that policy). The benefit is separate from the adult’s own CI sum assured and is usually subject to lower limits and specific eligibility rules.

  • Purpose: A contribution towards expenses arising from a child’s qualifying serious illness (e.g., time off work, travel, care costs).
  • Not a replacement for income: It’s typically a fixed lump sum and does not replace a parent’s earnings.
  • Policy-defined conditions: Cover only applies to conditions and severities stated in the policy wording.

Who Is Considered a “Child” for the Policy?

Definitions vary by insurer. Policies commonly cover the policyholder’s natural, adopted, or step-children within certain age ranges stated in the policy (for example, from 30 days old up to a maximum age such as 18 or 21, sometimes 22/23 in full-time education).

  • Minimum age: A newborn waiting period may apply (e.g., no cover for the first 30 days of life).
  • Maximum age: Cover usually ends at a specified age or when full-time education ends, whichever is earlier.
  • Residency: Policies may require UK residency (or set territorial limits) for claims to be valid.

How Much Does It Pay?

Children’s CI benefits are often capped. A common structure is either a fixed amount (for example, £25,000) or a percentage of the adult’s CI sum assured (for example, 25%), subject to an overall monetary maximum set by the insurer. Exact terms differ by policy.

  • Per-child and per-policy limits: Some policies cap claims per child, per condition, and/or in total across all children.
  • Multiple claims: Where permitted, multiple claims may be limited to different conditions and combined maximums.
  • Effect on adult cover: Children’s claims usually do not reduce the adult’s own CI sum assured, but always check the policy wording.

What Conditions Are Typically Covered?

Policies generally list specific conditions and definitions for children’s claims (which can be narrower or broader than adult definitions). Examples often include certain cancers, major organ failure, stroke, and serious neurological conditions — but exact lists, severities, and exclusions are determined by the insurer’s policy wording.

  • Severity and definitions: Some conditions require a specified stage or severity. A diagnosis alone may not be sufficient.
  • Congenital & pre-existing conditions: Many policies exclude congenital conditions present at birth and pre-existing conditions (see wording).
  • Waiting & survival periods: A minimum survival period following diagnosis may apply; waiting periods can also apply before cover starts.

Exclusions, Waiting Periods & Other Limits (Examples)

Exact terms vary by provider. Always refer to the policy document.

  • Exclusions for illnesses or events that occurred before the policy start date.
  • Exclusions related to non-disclosure or misrepresentation at application.
  • Territorial limits for treatment or residence outside specified regions.
  • Waiting periods after policy start or birth, and survival periods after diagnosis.
  • Limits on the number of claims and the total payable per policy.

Premiums & Cost Impact

Where children’s cover is optional, adding it can change the overall premium. Where it is built-in, the benefit is usually pre-priced by the insurer. Premiums for the main CI policy can depend on the adult applicant’s age, medical history, smoker status, occupation, and other risk factors assessed by the insurer.

Claims Overview (Information Only)

If a listed condition is diagnosed and the definition is met, the policyholder typically submits a claim with medical evidence. The insurer assesses the claim against the policy wording and, if valid, pays the children’s benefit to the policy owner (or as stated in the policy). Processing times vary by insurer and case complexity.

Key Points to Remember

  • Children’s cover is usually a limited, defined benefit with caps and exclusions.
  • Covered conditions, definitions, ages, waiting/survival periods, and claim limits vary by policy.
  • This guide is educational and does not assess suitability for any individual.

If you would like information about policy wording or processes from an insurer, consider contacting the provider directly or speaking with a qualified professional.
