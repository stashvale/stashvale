Choosing a Deferred Period

This guide explains, in plain English, what a deferred period is, how it works, and typical things people consider. This page is for information only and does not constitute financial advice.

What Is a Deferred Period?

The deferred period is the waiting time between when illness or injury stops you working and when an Income Protection (IP) benefit could begin to be paid, if a valid claim is accepted. Common options include 4, 8, 13, 26 or 52 weeks. Terms and availability vary by product.

Why Deferred Periods Exist

  • Helps align with other income: Some people receive employer sick pay for a period. IP can be set to start later so benefits do not overlap.
  • Price impact: As a general rule, longer deferred periods usually cost less than shorter ones because fewer short-term claims fall within cover.
  • Administration & clarity: A defined waiting time can make it clear when a potential benefit could begin if a claim is accepted.

Exact pricing and eligibility depend on the specific policy and underwriting.

How It Affects Cost & When Benefits Start

  • Short deferred periods (e.g., 4–8 weeks): Benefits may begin sooner after a valid claim is accepted, but premiums are typically higher.
  • Medium deferred periods (e.g., 13–26 weeks): A balance between price and waiting time for some people, depending on other income arrangements.
  • Long deferred periods (e.g., 52 weeks+): Lower premiums in many cases, but a long gap before any benefit could start.

General, Non-Advisory Considerations

  • Employer sick pay: Some contracts provide full or part pay for a period. People sometimes aim to align the deferred period to begin after that.
  • Statutory Sick Pay (SSP): In the UK, SSP may be payable for a limited time if eligible. It is usually lower than typical earnings.
  • Savings & emergency funds: A cash buffer can help bridge a waiting period, but circumstances differ.
  • Self-employed income patterns: Income variability and the absence of employer sick pay can influence how long a waiting period feels manageable.
  • Definition of incapacity: Policies can use different work-ability definitions (for example, “own occupation”). Definitions and exclusions affect claims.

This list is not exhaustive. Policy terms, definitions, exclusions and underwriting vary.

Simple Illustrative Examples (Not Advice)

  • Example A: Someone with 3 months’ employer sick pay and a small emergency fund might look at a ~13-week deferred period so potential benefits could start after pay reduces or stops.
  • Example B: A self-employed person with limited savings might prefer a shorter waiting time so the gap before a potential claim payment is smaller.

These examples are generic illustrations only. Individual needs and policy availability differ.

Key Points to Remember

  • A shorter deferred period usually costs more; a longer one usually costs less.
  • Aligning the waiting time with other income sources is a common approach people consider.
  • Product terms, definitions, exclusions and underwriting vary. Always read policy documents.
