Whole of Life Premiums — Guaranteed vs Reviewable

This guide explains the difference between guaranteed and reviewable premiums in whole of life policies. It is general information only — not advice.

What Are the Two Main Premium Types?

  • Guaranteed premiums: Set at the outset and designed to stay the same throughout the policy, as long as terms are met.
  • Reviewable premiums: Set initially, then reviewed at intervals set out in the policy (for example every 5 or 10 years). At review, the insurer may change premiums in line with policy rules.

Why Reviews Happen (High Level)

Reviewable premiums can change due to factors referenced in the policy, such as insurer assumptions, charges, longevity experience, or investment performance if benefits are linked to with-profits/unit-linked structures. The policy sets out the method and timing of review.

Exact mechanisms vary by provider and product. Always check the policy literature for how reviews are calculated.

Potential Pros & Cons (Information Only)

  • Guaranteed: Price certainty; usually higher initial premium than a comparable reviewable structure.
  • Reviewable: Often lower initial premium; future costs can rise or fall at review under policy rules, affecting affordability.

What If Premiums Change on Review?

Policies usually explain the options at review (for example, accept a revised premium; keep premiums the same and adjust benefits; or other options permitted by the product). The specific choices and implications depend on the contract.

Other Features that Can Affect Cost

  • Smoker status, age and underwriting: Risk factors influence initial price and, for reviewable plans, future projections under policy rules.
  • Benefit level: Higher sums assured typically mean higher premiums.
  • With-profits/unit-linked variants: Where benefits are linked to investments, bonus rates or unit values can influence outcomes under the policy’s method.

Illustrative Examples (Not Advice)

  • Example A: A person who prefers cost certainty may favour guaranteed premiums, understanding the higher fixed cost.
  • Example B: A person who prioritises lower initial cost may consider reviewable premiums, recognising costs may change at review.

These examples are generic to explain concepts only. Individual needs vary.

Key Points to Remember

  • Guaranteed = price certainty; Reviewable = price can change under policy rules.
  • Check the policy for review timing, methodology and available options at review.
  • Tax treatment and product features can change; professional advice may help with tax/estate planning.
