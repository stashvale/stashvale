SIPP Basics Explained

A Self-Invested Personal Pension (SIPP) is a type of UK personal pension that lets you choose from a broad range of investments within pension rules. This page explains the key ideas in plain English.

What is a SIPP?

A SIPP is a personal pension wrapper. Money paid into a SIPP is invested for retirement and is intended to be held long term. Providers offer different features, charges and investment choices. SIPPs sit alongside other pension types such as workplace pensions and stakeholder pensions.

How contributions generally work

  • You can usually contribute personally and, in some cases, via transfers from other pensions (subject to provider processes and rules).
  • There are UK-wide limits and conditions that apply to pension contributions and tax relief. These can change and may depend on earnings and other factors.
  • Employers may also contribute to certain personal pensions; provider terms apply.

Investment choice

Within a SIPP you can usually access a wide list of eligible investments (for example, funds and shares), depending on the provider. Investment risk varies; values can go down as well as up, and you may get back less than you invest.

Accessing your pension

Pension access is governed by legislation and provider processes. Access is usually limited until a specified minimum pension age and may involve tax considerations. Options can include taking a tax-free element (subject to limits) and taxable income; not all options are available from every provider.

How a SIPP differs from other pensions

  • Control: A SIPP often offers wider investment choice and more decision-making responsibility.
  • Ongoing management: You are responsible for monitoring and managing the account unless you appoint an adviser or discretionary manager.
  • Charges: Platform, dealing and fund charges apply and can vary.

Things to consider

  • Investments can fall as well as rise; future returns are not guaranteed.
  • Tax rules and pension legislation can change and may affect contributions and withdrawals.
  • Consolidating pensions, transferring, or accessing benefits can involve costs, risks and irreversible decisions.
